Help Children Navigate Their Emotional World

Summary:

Sesame Workshop, a leader in educational children's programming, sought to improve how young audiences emotionally engage with its content. Given Elmo’s central role in fostering emotional intelligence, the research aimed to understand whether third-party narration enhances children’s ability to recognize and connect with emotions depicted on screen.

The Problem

Sesame Street’s Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) initiative aimed to teach children emotional intelligence, but the content wasn’t as emotionally engaging as expected. Children were not as emotionally connected to the content as Sesame Workshop had hoped, especially during passive viewing of TV episodes. The task was to compare this traditional TV content with an interactive app experience to see which method would more effectively help children recognize and connect with emotions.


Research Process

To address this, we conducted research with 30+ children, focusing on their emotional reactions to SEL concepts. The research methods included:


User Interviews

Spoke directly with children to understand their emotional responses and preferences.

Playtesting

Designed and facilitated interactive sessions, including quizzes and third-person activities, to observe how children engaged with SEL content.

Interaction Analysis

Studied children’s behavior during activities to identify patterns in emotional engagement.

Qualitative Insights: Understanding Emotional Engagement in Kids Aged 4-6

To understand how children emotionally engage with Sesame Street’s Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) content, we observed their reactions during interactive playtesting sessions. Using iPads and TV screens, children watched Elmo and other characters while responding with thumbs up or thumbs down to express their feelings. Here’s what we learned:

“Elmo made me happy when he shared his toys!”

Thumbs Up: 85% of children gave a thumbs up when Elmo demonstrated sharing during a storyline.

Observed Behavior: Kids smiled, clapped, and imitated Elmo’s actions, showing higher emotional engagement during interactive, relatable scenarios.

“I didn’t like when Elmo was sad.”

Thumbs Down: 70% of children gave a thumbs down when Elmo was sad in a story.

Observed Behavior: Some children frowned, hugged their parents, or asked, “Why is Elmo sad?” This showed their emotional connection to the character and desire to see him happy.

“I like playing the quiz game with Elmo!”

Thumbs Up: 90% of children gave a thumbs up during interactive quizzes where they helped Elmo solve problems.

Observed Behavior: Kids leaned forward, pointed at the screen, and cheered when they got answers right, showing higher engagement with playful, interactive activities compared to passive watching.

Research Findings

  • Children responded better to interactive, playful activities than to passive learning methods.
  • Emotional engagement was higher when characters and storylines reflected children’s real-life experiences.
  • Quizzes and third-person activities were particularly effective in maintaining attention and fostering emotional connection.
  • Children who received third-party narration showed a 25% increase in emotional recognition compared to those who did not.

What If Career Exploration Felt Like A Video Game

Core Insight: Gen Z needs to experience careers, not just read about them


Our Game Plan:

Career Simulation Quests

Day-in-the-life simulations (e.g., "Be a Cybersecurity Agent" with real hacking puzzles)

AI-Powered Roadmap Builder

Turn vague aspirations into actionable steps with skill mapping (e.g., "Your debate club experience = 60% to Lawyer role")

But Why Create An Interactive Platform?

Based on research, it shows that students are more interested in a career search from online platform.


  • The COVID-19 pandemic has disrupted traditional career exploration opportunities (job shadowing & internship) making virtual resources such as career exploration websites more important than ever before
  • According to a survey by Junior Achievement USA, 71% of high school students said that the pandemic has made them more interested in exploring career options online.

From Chaos to Clarity: The Iteration Journey

Pivot Moment

Introduced gaming UI patterns after observing users spend 3X more time on Duolingo than our prototype.

Final Solution

Progress bars for career exploration completeness, achievement badges for skill development, avatar customization reflecting career paths.

Final Interface

Design of the career description interface, featuring progress bars to track career exploration. Users can visually assess their progress in exploring different career paths.

The AI-driven roadmap design, displaying personalized suggestions for users based on their career interests. Achievements and skill development are also tracked visually.

Gaming simulation interface, gamifying the career exploration process. Users are rewarded with achievement badges for completing milestones, enhancing engagement and learning.
